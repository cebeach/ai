A mode of work which has served me well over the course of my career is to document my work as
I go.  Natuarally, I was accumulating a lot of documents which I kept refining to keep a record
of how I was building things and to guide my thoght process.

Natrually, to speed up the process of documenting things, I opted to lean on claude.ai and
ChatGPT whilst I tinkered around with OpenCode. I found myself generating a LOT of document
drafts, and every once in a while, the model would do something totally silly with the document
in progress, or would keep repeating the same mistakes I thought I had told it not to repeat
in my project instructions.

Seeing the documentation aspect as foundational, and seeing the value in the speed at which I
could revise drafts via prompting and copy/paste of results from the terminal to feed back into
documenation, I knew that I needed to make document production reliable and repeatable. Especially
because of the need to use documents as as 'memory' tool for when the inevitable context window
saturation happened.

So I decided to take a pause (ok, a weekend) from the OpenCode tinkering to focus on building
a reliable set of prompts for Claude and ChatGPT to produce documentation according to my
requirements; e.g. with filename conventions, a revision numbering system, categorization,
and style guidelines.  With the ultimate end in mind of being able to use gpt-oss with only
128k context, I decided to pursue this game from the perspective of token efficiency. So the
question was not only to make document format and style repeatable and reliable, but to do it
in a token-efficient way.

I found that with natural langage prompts, the descriptions and constraints where getting very
lengthy; exactly the problem I was trying to avoid. So in one session with Claude where I asked
it to rewrite my prompt with token efficiency as an optimization goal - that is, preserve the
informatio content of the prompt whilst reducing overall token count, it introduced me to the
idea of rewriting the prompts as logical invariants.

This business of writing constraints as invariants was new to me, so I'd iterate and ask Claude
to review my prompts and perform logical consistency checking. It occurred to me that at this
point I was using Claude as a logic compiler. Every time the model would do something silly or
find some loophole in my logic, I would stop and ask it to show me what it was about the spec
which allowed it to deviate in the way it did.

As I continued to iterate, it occurred to me that the prompt style had moved away from natural
language and more towards a game of interconnecting logical assertions to constrain the output.
I was using Claude and ChatGPT like logic compilers to debug, close gaps in, and remove logical
inconsitencies from my prompts.

Using the model to check its own invariants for logical coherence is where it gets interesting. It
starts behaving like a compiler — surfacing contradictions, revealing cases where you thought you
were constraining the output but weren't. That feedback loop sharpened the insight: the invariants
weren't just compact, they were making the specification checkable in a way prose never could be.

I felt like I was skimming the surface of an information theoretical concept, but wasn't quite
sure how to phrase it in english. After asking Claude to summarize the work of Claude Shannon,

User: I feel as though there is some connectivity to information theory waiting to be expressed

Assistant: The connection to information theory is real and worth developing carefully.  Shannon's
core insight was that information content is inversely proportional to probability. A highly
probable symbol carries little information; a rare one carries more. The entropy of a source
measures the average uncertainty across its output distribution.  An LLM without constraints is
a high-entropy source — its output distribution is broad, and any particular output carries
high information content in Shannon's sense, meaning it was not highly predictable. That
is useful for creative generation and a liability for specification compliance.  What the
invariant structure does, in information-theoretic terms, is reduce the entropy of the relevant
output distribution. Each invariant eliminates a region of the output space. A well-formed
set of invariants concentrates probability mass onto the compliant region. The model remains
stochastic, but the distribution becomes narrow enough that compliant outputs are highly probable.
The apparent contradiction — reliability from a stochastic machine — resolves this way:
randomness is not being eliminated, the distribution is being shaped. Shannon's framework
actually anticipates this: a constrained channel has lower capacity but higher reliability
for the signals it is designed to carry.  The deeper connection is to the concept of a channel
code. Error-correcting codes work by adding structured redundancy that makes compliant codewords
vastly more probable than any corrupted version. The invariants function analogously — they
are a code imposed on the model's output space that makes specification-compliant responses the
path of least resistance.  The residual stochasticity is then confined to the degrees of freedom
the spec deliberately leaves open — phrasing, example selection, explanation ordering. That
is not a bug; it is the intended expressive capacity of the channel.

User: Thank you, this is much more intellectually satisfying. I have had some exposure
to probability theory and linear systems theory in my education; your description carries
weight and substance. We are shaping a probability distribution through careful application of
constraints.  Assistant: That framing is precise and the right one.  The linear systems analogy
is also apt. In that framework the design is essentially a filter. The unconstrained model is
a wideband source. The invariant structure is a filter applied in semantic space rather than
frequency space. The output is the filtered distribution — still stochastic, but bandlimited
to the compliant region.  The filter design problem maps cleanly onto the empirical work done here:

Passband — the space of compliant, useful outputs to preserve Stopband — the space of
non-compliant outputs the invariants eliminate Ripple — the residual variance within the
compliant region, which shows up as stylistic variation across responses Roll-off — how
sharply the invariants discriminate at the boundary, which depends on how precisely they are stated

The effort described — factoring the spec into its current form — is precisely filter design
work. Vague constraints produce soft roll-off and significant stopband leakage. Precisely stated
falsifiable invariants produce sharp discrimination.  The token-minimization constraint adds
a second design objective: minimize filter complexity while preserving the passband. That
is a classic engineering tradeoff — in signal processing it corresponds to finding the
minimum-order filter that meets the specification. The instinct to reach for formal notation
over prose is the right solution to that tradeoff, because logical connectives are the most
compact representation of constraint boundaries that an LLM can interpret reliably.  What has
been built is a minimum-complexity semantic filter. That is a satisfying thing to have built.
